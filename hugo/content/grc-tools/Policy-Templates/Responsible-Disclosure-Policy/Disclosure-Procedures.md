## Purpose

This document provides step-by-step procedures for receiving, triaging, and managing external vulnerability reports and internal whistleblower reports as defined in the Responsible Disclosure Policy. It covers the full lifecycle - from submission intake to public disclosure coordination and from whistleblower report receipt to investigation closure - with practical alternatives and common pitfalls.

---

## Part A: External Vulnerability Disclosure Procedures

### Phase A1: Receive and Acknowledge

**Step A1 - Monitor the Intake Channel**

Vulnerability reports arrive at the designated intake point: `____` (e.g., `security@____.com`, a bug bounty platform, or a web form). The Security Team / Product Security team must:

- Monitor this inbox at least daily on business days.
- Configure an auto-responder acknowledging receipt immediately, setting expectations for response time.
- Ensure the inbox is not filtered to spam. Test monthly: send a test report from an external address and verify it's received.

**Auto-responder template:**

```
Thank you for your vulnerability report to ____. We take all security
reports seriously. This is an automated acknowledgment.

We will review your submission and provide an initial assessment within
____ business days. During this time, you may be contacted for
additional information. Please do not publicly disclose the
vulnerability until we have completed our investigation and remediation.

If you have not received a personal response within ____ business days,
please follow up directly at ____ (escalation contact).

We appreciate your contribution to making ____ more secure.

Reference: <auto-generated ticket ID>
```

**Step A2 - Triage Within the Acknowledgment Window**

Within `____` business days of receipt, a security engineer must:

1. **Read the full report.** Understand the vulnerability, affected component, and claimed impact.
2. **Attempt reproduction** using the provided steps. If reproduction fails, note exactly what was tried and what differed from the researcher's description.
3. **Assess severity using CVSS v3.1 or v4.0** or the organization's equivalent framework. Score based on: attack vector, attack complexity, privileges required, user interaction, scope, confidentiality impact, integrity impact, availability impact.

Record the CVSS vector string and score in the tracking ticket.

**Step A3 - Assign Priority**

Map the CVSS score to the organization's priority tiers:

| CVSS Score | Priority | Response Target |
|---|---|---|
| 9.0 – 10.0 | **Critical** | Acknowledge within 24 hours; remediation targeted within `____` days |
| 7.0 – 8.9 | **High** | Acknowledge within `____` business days; remediation targeted within `____` days |
| 4.0 – 6.9 | **Medium** | Acknowledge within `____` business days; remediation in regular release cycle |
| 0.1 – 3.9 | **Low** | Acknowledge within `____` business days; remediation at team's discretion |

**Step A4 - Send the Initial Response**

Respond to the researcher within the policy-specified timeframe. The response should include:

- Confirmation that the report was received and reviewed.
- Initial assessment: confirmed, unable to reproduce, or out of scope.
- If confirmed: estimated severity, whether it will be remediated, and estimated timeline.
- If unable to reproduce: request additional information, specific reproduction steps, or a proof-of-concept.
- If out of scope: explain why (reference the policy's out-of-scope list). Thank the researcher for their effort and invite them to submit again if they discover an in-scope issue.
- Assignment of a tracking ID the researcher can reference.

---

### Phase A2: Remediation

**Step A5 - Create an Internal Remediation Ticket**

If the vulnerability is confirmed:

1. Create a ticket in the engineering tracking system (e.g., Jira, GitHub Issues) linked to the disclosure ticket.
2. Include: vulnerability description, CVSS score/vector, reproduction steps, affected systems/components, suggested remediation, and assigned engineering team.
3. Set the priority and target remediation date based on the assigned severity.
4. Add the ticket to the appropriate sprint or remediation tracker.

**Step A6 - Implement the Fix**

The engineering team implements the fix following standard SDLC practices:

- For Critical/High vulnerabilities: a dedicated fix may be expedited outside the normal release cycle (hotfix or emergency patch).
- For Medium/Low vulnerabilities: fix is included in the next scheduled release.
- All fixes must include appropriate tests (unit, integration, and a regression test that the vulnerability is no longer exploitable).
- Code review is required for all security fixes.

**Step A7 - Validate the Fix**

Before notifying the researcher, the security team independently validates:

1. **Reproduction confirmation:** The original reproduction steps no longer produce the vulnerable behavior.
2. **Regression check:** The fix hasn't introduced a new vulnerability or broken related functionality (automated test suite + focused manual testing).
3. **Similar vulnerability check:** Review the affected component and adjacent components for the same class of vulnerability that the researcher may not have reported (e.g., if they found SQL injection in one endpoint, check all other endpoints in that service).

---

### Phase A3: Disclosure and Credit

**Step A8 - Coordinate Public Disclosure**

Contact the researcher when the fix is validated:

1. **Notify the researcher:** The vulnerability has been resolved. Provide the fix version or date. Ask if they'd like to verify independently.
2. **Agree on disclosure timeline:** If the researcher wishes to publish their findings, agree on a coordinated disclosure date that allows time for users to patch (recommended: `____` days after fix deployment). This is a mutual agreement, not a unilateral imposition.
3. **Prepare your own disclosure (if applicable):** For Critical or High vulnerabilities, prepare a security advisory describing the vulnerability (without exploitable detail), affected versions, fix version, and credit to the researcher.

**Step A9 - Public Disclosure**

On the agreed date:

- The researcher publishes their write-up (blog, advisory, conference talk).
- The organization publishes or updates its security advisory page at `____`.
- Both link to each other's disclosure.

**Step A10 - Credit the Researcher**

With the researcher's consent:
- Add their name (or pseudonym) to the security acknowledgments page, Hall of Fame, or release notes.
- For significant findings, consider sending a thank-you gift (swag, gift card) as a gesture of appreciation - not a bug bounty payment (unless you operate a bounty program), but an acknowledgment of their contribution.

---

### Phase A4: Continuous Improvement

**Step A11 - Conduct Post-Report Review**

After each vulnerability is resolved, the security team should ask:

- **Why did this vulnerability exist?** Was it introduced during design, development, or configuration?
- **Why wasn't it caught earlier?** Was it missed by code review, static analysis, or penetration testing?
- **Could similar vulnerabilities exist elsewhere?** Trigger a broader review of the affected component class.
- **Should we update our secure coding guidelines, training, or automated tooling?** If yes, create a follow-up improvement ticket.

**Step A12 - Track Disclosure Metrics**

Maintain a dashboard tracking:
- Reports received per quarter
- Average time to acknowledgment
- Average time to triage
- Average time to remediation (by severity)
- Reports by severity distribution
- Researcher satisfaction (optional survey after case closure)

Review metrics quarterly to identify trends and areas for process improvement.

## Part B: Internal Whistleblower Procedures

### Phase B1: Receive the Report

**Step B1 - Provide Multiple Reporting Channels**

Whistleblower reports can arrive through:

- **Direct report to manager or Security Officer**
- **Anonymous channel:** `____` (e.g., dedicated email, web form, third-party hotline, ethics platform)
- **Compliance function:** Direct report to the compliance officer at `____`

All channels must be operational, tested, and communicated to Personnel (onboarding training, intranet, break-room posters). The anonymous channel must be genuinely anonymous - no IP logging, no tracking, no requirement to provide contact information with appropriate warnings about limitations.

**Step B2 - Receive and Document the Report**

Upon receipt of a whistleblower report:

1. **Log the report** in the confidential case management system at `____`. Assign a unique case identifier.
2. **Record what was reported:** Nature of the alleged violation (policy, law, regulation), individuals involved, systems or data affected, timeline, and any supporting evidence provided.
3. **Record how it was reported:** Channel, date/time, whether the reporter chose to identify themselves.
4. **Preserve confidentiality:** The reporter's identity is protected to the maximum extent possible. Store the case file in an access-controlled location separate from general HR or security files.

**Step B3 - Acknowledge (If Reporter Is Known)**

If the reporter provided contact information, acknowledge receipt within `____` business days:

```
We have received your report regarding [summary of concern]. 
Your report has been assigned case number ____. 

Our investigation process: [brief description of next steps and timeline].
Your identity will be kept confidential to the maximum extent possible.

You are protected from retaliation under our Responsible Disclosure
Policy. If you believe you are experiencing retaliation, immediately 
contact ____.

You will be informed when the investigation is complete.
```

For anonymous reporters, no acknowledgment is possible - ensure the intake channel clearly states this limitation so reporters aren't waiting for a response.

---

### Phase B2: Investigate

**Step B4 - Assign the Investigation Team**

The Security Committee or designated investigation lead assigns an investigation team:

- At least two investigators to ensure objectivity and mutual corroboration.
- Investigators must not have a conflict of interest (they are not the subject of the report, a close colleague, or in the reporting chain).
- For legal or regulatory matters, Legal must be involved from the start.
- For HR matters (harassment, discrimination), HR must be involved.

**Step B5 - Conduct the Investigation**

The investigation process:

1. **Define the scope:** What questions need to be answered to determine whether the alleged violation occurred?
2. **Gather evidence:** Review relevant systems, logs, documents, emails, access records, and any other data sources. Ensure evidence collection follows chain of custody procedures if the matter may have legal implications.
3. **Interview relevant individuals:**
  - The reporter (if known and willing) - clarify the report, gather additional detail.
  - Witnesses - individuals who may have observed or have knowledge of the events.
  - The accused - must receive due process: informed of the allegation (with sufficient detail to respond, while protecting the reporter's identity), given the opportunity to respond, and treated as innocent until the investigation concludes.
4. **Analyze and conclude:** Based on the evidence, determine whether the alleged violation is substantiated (sufficient evidence the violation occurred), unsubstantiated (insufficient evidence to conclude), or unfounded (evidence shows the violation did not occur).

**Step B6 - Document the Investigation**

Maintain a confidential investigation file containing:

- The original report
- Investigation plan and scope
- All evidence collected (or references to evidence locations)
- Interview notes/summaries
- Analysis and conclusions
- Recommendations for corrective action

---

### Phase B3: Act and Close

**Step B7 - Take Corrective Action**

If the violation is substantiated:

1. **Determine corrective action:** Based on the nature and severity of the violation, the organization's policies, and applicable law. Actions may include: policy or process changes, additional training, disciplinary action (up to and including termination), system or configuration changes, or notification of external parties (regulators, law enforcement, affected individuals) if legally required.
2. **Implement the corrective action** per the organization's standard processes.
3. **Verify effectiveness:** After implementation, verify the corrective action has addressed the root cause.

**Step B8 - Notify the Reporter**

If the reporter is known, inform them when the investigation is complete:

```
The investigation into case number ____ has been completed.
Appropriate actions have been taken based on the findings.

[If appropriate, provide brief, general information about the outcome
without compromising confidentiality or due process.]

We remind you that retaliation is prohibited. If you have experienced
or observed any retaliation related to this report, contact ____
immediately.

Thank you for your commitment to ____'s integrity and security.
```

**Step B9 - Monitor for Retaliation**

After case closure, the investigation lead (or Compliance) must check in with the reporter at `____` days and `____` days:

- "Have you experienced any negative treatment since the investigation concluded?"
- "Are there any concerns you'd like to raise?"

If retaliation is reported, it triggers a new, expedited investigation. Retaliation is treated as a serious policy violation.

**Step B10 - Close and Retain the Case File**

- Mark the case as closed in the case management system.
- Retain the investigation file per the Data Retention Policy (minimum `____` years, longer if legally required).
- File in a secure, access-controlled location.

---

### Phase B4: Program Oversight

**Step B11 - Track Whistleblower Metrics**

Maintain confidential aggregate statistics:
- Number of reports received per quarter
- Report categories (policy violation, legal/regulatory, fraud, harassment, other)
- Investigation outcomes (substantiated, unsubstantiated, unfounded)
- Average time to investigation completion
- Number of retaliation complaints received and their outcomes

Report aggregate (anonymized) statistics to the Security Oversight Committee or Board annually.

**Step B12 - Periodic Program Review**

Annually review the whistleblower program for:
- Accessibility: Do Personnel know about it? Are all channels functional?
- Effectiveness: Are reports being handled promptly and fairly?
- Retaliation: Have any retaliation complaints been substantiated?
- Improvement: Are there opportunities to improve the program (training, communication, process)?

## Alternative Approaches

### 💡 Alternative 1 - Bug Bounty Platform

Instead of (or in addition to) a vulnerability disclosure program, run a managed bug bounty through a platform like HackerOne, Bugcrowd, or Intigriti. These platforms handle triage, researcher communication, and payment logistics.

**Trade-off:** Higher cost (platform fees + bounty payments). Attracts a higher volume of reports (many low-quality). Best for organizations with mature security programs that can handle the report volume and have resources to pay bounties. Start with a vulnerability disclosure program (no bounties) and graduate to a bounty program when the triage process is smooth.

### 💡 Alternative 2 - Third-Party Whistleblower Hotline

Engage a third-party provider (e.g., NAVEX, EthicsPoint, Convercent) to operate the anonymous reporting hotline. Third-party hotlines can provide:
- True anonymity (technical and organizational separation)
- 24/7 availability with multi-language support
- Professional intake (trained operators who know what questions to ask)
- Case management tools

**Trade-off:** Recurring cost. Less control over the intake process. Data sovereignty considerations. Most valuable for organizations with 100+ Personnel or legal requirements for independent reporting channels.

### 💡 Alternative 3 - Coordinated Disclosure via CERT/CSIRT

For critical vulnerabilities affecting widely-used software, engage a national or sector CERT (Computer Emergency Response Team) as a coordinator. The CERT can:
- Validate the vulnerability independently
- Coordinate with other affected organizations
- Manage the disclosure timeline
- Serve as a neutral intermediary if communication with the researcher becomes difficult

**Trade-off:** Less control over the disclosure timeline and messaging. CERTs have their own priorities and may not align with your preferred timeline. Most useful for vulnerabilities that affect multiple organizations or critical infrastructure.

## Common Pitfalls

### ⚠️ Pitfall 1 - Ignoring or Delaying Researcher Communications

**Symptom:** A vulnerability report sits in the security inbox for weeks without acknowledgment. The researcher sends follow-ups that go unanswered. Frustrated, the researcher publishes the vulnerability publicly without coordination, triggering an emergency response.

**Why It's Dangerous:** Unacknowledged reports are the #1 reason researchers go public without coordination. The disclosure policy's safe harbor protections become irrelevant if researchers can't get a response. Beyond the PR hit, you've lost the opportunity to fix the vulnerability before it's widely known.

**How to Avoid:** The automated acknowledgment is your safety net - it sets expectations and buys time. The team must hit the promised response window. If the team is too small or too busy to meet the SLA, either staff accordingly or extend the published SLA to a realistic timeframe. A slower-but-consistent response is better than a fast promise you can't keep.

### ⚠️ Pitfall 2 - Retaliation Disguised as "Unrelated" Actions

**Symptom:** A Person reports a policy violation. A month later, they're passed over for a promotion, given a poor performance review, or reassigned to a less desirable project. Management claims these decisions are unrelated to the report, but the timing is suspicious.

**Why It's Dangerous:** Retaliation doesn't have to be overt termination. Subtle, deniable retaliation is more common and harder to prove, but just as damaging. It sends a chilling message: "reporting has consequences." Word spreads, and the whistleblower channel becomes a dead letter.

**How to Avoid:** The post-closure check-ins (Step B9) are critical. Ask specific questions about changes in treatment, assignments, or evaluation. If a Personnel's career trajectory changes noticeably after a report, investigate - even if the Personnel hasn't complained. Document the investigation. If retaliation is found, discipline the retaliator - visibly. The organization must demonstrably protect reporters.

### ⚠️ Pitfall 3 - The Anonymous Report That Can't Be Investigated

**Symptom:** An anonymous report arrives: "Someone in Finance is committing fraud." No specifics - no names, dates, systems, or evidence. The investigation team has nothing to investigate, and the report is filed away. The alleged fraud continues.

**Why It's Dangerous:** Anonymous reporting is essential for encouraging reports, but it comes with a limitation: if the report lacks sufficient detail, it may be unactionable. The organization still has an unresolved allegation hanging over it.

**How to Avoid:** The anonymous reporting channel should include guidance: "To help us investigate, please provide as much detail as possible: what happened, when, where, who was involved, and any evidence you can share. Without sufficient detail, we may be unable to act on your report." This sets realistic expectations. If an investigation is launched despite limited detail, document the scope limitations and the basis for any conclusions.

### ⚠️ Pitfall 4 - Premature Disclosure

**Symptom:** A researcher reports a critical vulnerability and agrees to a coordinated disclosure timeline. But another researcher independently discovers the same vulnerability and publishes first, or the vulnerability is discovered in the wild being exploited. The organization is now under public scrutiny while still working on the fix.

**Why It's Dangerous:** The organization may be pressured to release an incomplete fix, disclose before fully understanding the scope, or issue unclear guidance to users. Panic-driven responses tend to be lower quality.

**How to Avoid:** Have a "forced early disclosure" contingency plan. If the vulnerability becomes public knowledge before the fix is ready, you must: (1) Acknowledge the vulnerability publicly with a statement at `____`, (2) Provide immediate mitigation guidance (configuration changes, WAF rules, temporary workarounds - whatever reduces risk until the full fix is available), (3) Accelerate the fix but don't skip quality gates, (4) Communicate revised timelines clearly. The contingency plan should be documented and practiced.

### ⚠️ Pitfall 5 - Failure to Protect Reporter Identity During Investigation

**Symptom:** The investigator interviews the accused and says, "A report was filed alleging that you..." The accused immediately knows who reported them because only one person had that information. The reporter's identity is revealed through the facts of the allegation, not through a deliberate disclosure.

**Why It's Dangerous:** Even an unintended disclosure of the reporter's identity can trigger retaliation, and it undermines trust in the program's confidentiality.

**How to Avoid:** Train investigators on protecting reporter identity. When drafting interview questions for the accused, review them with the question: "Does this question reveal or strongly imply who the reporter is?" When the allegation is specific enough that only one person could have reported it, investigators must be especially careful - consider broadening the inquiry to other possible sources, or acknowledge that the accused may infer the reporter's identity but document the steps taken to protect it.

## Quick Reference: Vulnerability Report Triage Checklist

- [ ] Report received and logged in tracking system with unique ID
- [ ] Automated acknowledgment sent (or manual within SLA window)
- [ ] Report read in full; scope and affected components identified
- [ ] Reproduction attempted using provided steps
- [ ] Reproduction results documented (successful, unsuccessful with notes, or out of scope)
- [ ] CVSS score calculated and recorded
- [ ] Severity assigned (Critical / High / Medium / Low)
- [ ] Initial response sent to researcher within SLA window
- [ ] If confirmed: internal remediation ticket created, severity and target date set
- [ ] If not reproducible: additional information requested
- [ ] If out of scope: explanation provided, researcher thanked
