# Charter for Oversight Responsibility of Internal Control

## What This Is

This Charter formally establishes who is responsible for overseeing internal controls at the organization. It defines the relationship between the governing body (Board, Audit Committee) and executive management for security, risk, and compliance oversight. SOC 2 auditors expect to see this.

## What It Covers

- Establishment of an oversight body and its scope
- Responsibilities of the oversight body vs. management
- Meeting cadence and reporting requirements
- Resource allocation for security programs
- POA&M tracking for control deficiencies

## Document Structure

This folder contains three files:

- **`Charter-Template.md`** - The policy. Defines WHAT is required.
- **`Procedure.md`** - Companion procedures. Describes HOW to implement the policy.
- **`README.md`** - This overview.

The policy and procedure are deliberately separate: the policy is for all employees and auditors; the procedure is for implementers. When updating this policy, ensure implementation changes flow into the procedure document.

## Gotchas People Get Wrong

**1. Writing it and never following it.** The meeting cadence you specify ("quarterly" or "semi-annually") must actually happen. Auditors ask for meeting minutes. If the Charter says quarterly and you met twice last year, that's a finding.

**2. Confusing oversight with operations.** The Board/Audit Committee oversees - they don't operate. They review reports, approve strategies, and hold management accountable. If your Charter makes it sound like the Board is running the security program, it's wrong.

**3. Missing POA&M tracking.** The Plan of Action and Milestones is how you prove the oversight body actually did something about identified deficiencies. Without it, oversight looks performative.

**4. Not naming specific roles.** "Senior management" is vague. Name the specific roles (CISO, CFO, CEO) and what each is responsible for. Vague role assignments = audit findings.

**5. No escalation path.** What happens when management disagrees with the oversight body? What if the CISO can't get resources? Define the escalation path to the full Board or equivalent.

## Implementation Advice

- **Tie this to your annual review cycle.** The Charter's meeting cadence should align with your policy review cycle, risk assessment schedule, and audit timeline. Don't create a separate cadence that conflicts.
- **Keep meeting minutes.** Every oversight body meeting needs documented minutes with attendance, decisions, and action items. A template makes this easier.
- **This is often the first document an auditor requests.** Have it ready, signed, and dated.
- **The oversight body needs at least one person with security expertise.** If your Board has no security experience, document that they receive expert briefings or have an advisory relationship.
