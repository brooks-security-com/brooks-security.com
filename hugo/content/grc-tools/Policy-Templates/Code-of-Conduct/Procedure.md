# Code of Conduct - Implementation Procedures

> **Companion to:** Code of Conduct (Template.md)
> **Purpose:** How to implement the requirements. The policy defines WHAT; this describes HOW.

## Procedure 1: Annual Acknowledgment Process
### Standard Approach
1. Schedule the annual acknowledgment campaign to launch at the same time each year (e.g., January, or aligned with the fiscal year start).
2. Prepare the acknowledgment package: the current version of the Code of Conduct, a summary of any changes from the prior year, and the acknowledgment form (electronic or paper).
3. Distribute the acknowledgment via the organization's HRIS, compliance platform, or e-signature tool (e.g., DocuSign, HelloSign, BambooHR). The distribution must reach 100% of active Personnel.
4. Set a completion deadline (e.g., 30 calendar days from distribution).
5. Track completion rates weekly. At 7 days before the deadline, send a reminder to non-responders. At 3 days before, escalate to managers. At the deadline, escalate non-responders to HR for follow-up.
6. For new hires, include the Code acknowledgment in the onboarding checklist. The acknowledgment must be completed before system access is provisioned.
7. Retain signed acknowledgments in the employee personnel file or compliance document repository for the duration of employment plus the retention period specified in the Data Retention Policy.
8. After the campaign closes, produce a completion report: total Personnel, total acknowledged, total non-respondents, and any reported conflicts of interest disclosed during acknowledgment.
### Alternative Approaches
> **💡 Why you might choose differently:**
> - **Rolling acknowledgment tied to hire date:** Instead of a single annual campaign, require each individual to re-acknowledge on their hire anniversary. This distributes the workload across the year but requires more sophisticated tracking.
> - **Integrated with annual security awareness training:** Bundle the Code acknowledgment as a module within the annual security awareness training platform. Completion of the training module constitutes acknowledgment, reducing a separate workflow.
### Common Pitfalls
> **⚠️ Watch out:**
> - **"Acknowledged" without "read."** If the acknowledgment is a single-click checkbox buried in a long form, people click through without reading. Use a platform that requires scrolling through the full Code before the acknowledgment button activates, or include a brief quiz (2–3 questions) to confirm comprehension.
> - **Outdated Code version in the acknowledgment package.** Always verify that the Code linked in the acknowledgment is the current approved version. Distributing last year's Code for this year's acknowledgment is an audit finding.
> - **Contractors and temporary workers excluded.** The Code applies to contractors and temporary workers. Ensure they are included in the acknowledgment campaign or have a separate equivalent process managed through vendor management.

## Procedure 2: Conflict-of-Interest Disclosure
### Standard Approach
1. Maintain a Conflict-of-Interest (COI) disclosure form as a standalone document or integrated into the annual Code acknowledgment.
2. The disclosure form must prompt for: outside employment or advisory roles, board positions, significant investments in competitors/suppliers/partners, business dealings involving family or close relationships, gifts or entertainment exceeding a defined value threshold, and any other situation that could reasonably appear to create a conflict.
3. All Personnel must complete a COI disclosure annually, concurrent with the Code acknowledgment.
4. New hires complete the COI disclosure as part of onboarding. Personnel must also submit an updated disclosure within 30 days of any material change in circumstances.
5. Submitted disclosures are reviewed by the Compliance or Legal team within 10 business days.
6. For each disclosure that raises a potential conflict:
   a. Document the nature of the conflict.
   b. Assess the severity and materiality.
   c. Determine the resolution: (i) no action - no actual conflict exists, (ii) management plan - restrictions on certain activities or decisions, (iii) recusal - individual excluded from specific matters, or (iv) remediation - divestiture, resignation from outside role, or termination of the conflicting relationship.
   d. Communicate the resolution in writing to the individual and their manager.
   e. File the disclosure and resolution in the compliance records.
7. Maintain a COI register summarizing all active disclosures, resolutions, and review dates. Present a summary to the Oversight Body annually.
### Alternative Approaches
> **💡 Why you might choose differently:**
> - **Third-party COI management platform:** For larger organizations, use a dedicated COI management platform that automates disclosure collection, conflict scoring, and workflow routing.
> - **Role-specific disclosure thresholds:** For roles with purchasing authority or vendor selection responsibility, require more frequent disclosure (quarterly) and a lower gift threshold. For roles without financial decision authority, annual disclosure may suffice.
### Common Pitfalls
> **⚠️ Watch out:**
> - **Disclosures that sit unreviewed.** A submitted disclosure that no one reviews for 6 months is worse than no disclosure at all - it creates a record of knowledge without action. Establish and monitor a review SLA.
> - **"It's obvious, I didn't think I needed to disclose."** Personnel often fail to disclose conflicts they consider minor or obvious. The COI form must explicitly state: "When in doubt, disclose. Self-disclosure is never penalized; failure to disclose is."
> - **No mechanism for anonymous disclosure.** Some Personnel may be uncomfortable disclosing a conflict directly. Provide an anonymous channel (whistleblower hotline, anonymous web form) as an alternative.

## Procedure 3: Reporting and Escalation of Violations
### Standard Approach
1. Maintain multiple reporting channels: direct manager, Human Resources, Compliance/Legal team, and an anonymous reporting mechanism (whistleblower hotline, third-party service, or anonymous web form).
2. Publicize the reporting channels in the employee handbook, on the intranet, in onboarding materials, and during annual training. Personnel must know HOW to report without having to ask.
3. All reports of potential Code violations must be acknowledged within 1 business day and logged in a confidential case management system.
4. Triage each report:
  - **Workplace conduct complaints** (harassment, discrimination, bullying) → Human Resources.
  - **Conflicts of interest, financial integrity, legal compliance** → Compliance/Legal.
  - **Information security violations** → Security team, with HR/Compliance notified.
  - **Executive or senior management conduct** → Oversight Body Chair or Board.
5. Notify the reporting individual (if not anonymous) that the report has been received and is being reviewed. Do not promise specific outcomes.
6. Protect the confidentiality of the reporter and any witnesses to the extent permitted by law and consistent with the need to investigate.
7. Prohibit and monitor for retaliation. Any report of retaliation must be handled as a separate, priority investigation.
### Alternative Approaches
> **💡 Why you might choose differently:**
> - **Third-party whistleblower hotline:** If the organization lacks the resources or independence to manage reports internally, contract with a third-party hotline provider. This increases trust in anonymity and removes internal bias concerns.
> - **Ombudsperson model:** For larger organizations, designate an ombudsperson who receives reports, provides confidential guidance, and can mediate resolutions without formal investigation.
### Common Pitfalls
> **⚠️ Watch out:**
> - **Reporting channels that don't work.** Periodically test every reporting channel - call the hotline, submit an anonymous form, email the compliance alias. Verify that a real person receives and responds. A non-working reporting channel discovered during litigation is indefensible.
> - **Retaliation that is subtle.** Retaliation isn't always termination - it can be exclusion from meetings, reassignment to less desirable projects, negative performance reviews, or social ostracism. Train managers on what retaliation looks like and investigate all reports of adverse treatment following a Code report.
> - **No reporting for contractors.** Contractors need access to the same reporting channels. If the hotline is only accessible from the corporate network or HRIS, contractors are effectively silenced.

## Procedure 4: Investigation Process
### Standard Approach
1. Upon receipt of a report warranting investigation, the responsible function (HR, Compliance, or Legal) assigns an investigator within 2 business days.
2. The investigator must be independent - no personal or reporting relationship with the subject of the investigation. For senior management investigations, engage external counsel.
3. Develop an investigation plan: scope, allegations to be investigated, witnesses to interview, documents to review, estimated timeline.
4. Preserve all relevant evidence immediately: emails, chat logs, system access records, badge logs, documents. Issue a legal hold if the matter may result in litigation.
5. Conduct interviews:
   a. Interview the reporter first to understand the full scope of allegations.
   b. Interview witnesses with relevant knowledge.
   c. Interview the subject of the investigation last, after facts have been gathered.
   d. Document all interviews with contemporaneous notes. Inform interviewees of the expectation of confidentiality and the non-retaliation policy.
6. Analyze evidence and make a determination: (a) substantiated - violation occurred, (b) unsubstantiated - insufficient evidence, (c) unfounded - allegation determined to be false, or (d) inconclusive - cannot be determined.
7. Document findings in a written investigation report: allegations, evidence reviewed, interviews conducted, factual findings, determination, and recommended actions.
8. Communicate the outcome:
   a. To the reporter (if known): whether the investigation is complete and whether appropriate action was taken (without disclosing confidential disciplinary details).
   b. To the subject: the determination and any resulting action.
   c. To relevant stakeholders (HR, manager, executive) on a need-to-know basis.
9. Implement recommended actions (disciplinary action, policy change, training, remediation).
10. Close the case in the case management system with all documentation retained per the Data Retention Policy.
### Alternative Approaches
> **💡 Why you might choose differently:**
> - **External investigation for high-risk matters:** For allegations involving C-suite executives, financial fraud, or potential criminal conduct, engage external counsel or a forensic investigation firm to ensure independence and privilege protection.
> - **Triage committee for initial review:** Instead of a single person determining whether an investigation is warranted, convene a triage committee (HR, Legal, Compliance) to review reports and decide on the appropriate response.
### Common Pitfalls
> **⚠️ Watch out:**
> - **Investigation that takes too long.** An investigation that drags on for months leaves all parties in limbo, increases the risk of evidence loss, and signals that the organization doesn't take the matter seriously. Set a target completion timeline (e.g., 30 calendar days for standard cases, 14 days for cases involving workplace safety) and escalate if the timeline is at risk.
> - **Inconsistent discipline for similar violations.** Two employees commit the same Code violation; one is terminated, the other receives a written warning. This creates legal exposure for discrimination. Maintain a disciplinary precedent log to ensure consistency, and document the rationale for any deviation.
> - **Investigation report that draws legal conclusions.** The investigation report should state factual findings, not legal conclusions ("the evidence shows the employee accessed files without authorization" not "the employee committed a crime"). Legal conclusions invite defamation claims and may waive privilege.
