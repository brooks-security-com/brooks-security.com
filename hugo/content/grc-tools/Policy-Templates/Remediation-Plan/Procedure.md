# Remediation Plan - Implementation Procedures

> **Companion to:** Remediation Plan (Remediation-Template.md)
> **Purpose:** How to implement the requirements. The policy defines WHAT; this describes HOW.

## Procedure 1: Finding → Ticket Workflow
### Standard Approach
1. Findings enter the remediation pipeline from any detection source:
   a. **Automated scanners** (vulnerability scanner, CSPM, SAST/DAST, dependency scanner) → auto-create ticket via API integration.
   b. **Manual discovery** (penetration test, audit, security review, bug bounty) → security analyst manually creates ticket using a standardized template.
   c. **Incident post-mortem** → findings from incident root cause analysis are created as remediation tickets and linked to the incident record.
2. Each finding ticket must contain the minimum required information:
   a. Unique identifier (auto-generated by the ticketing system).
   b. Finding description: what was found, where, detection method, and date of discovery.
   c. Severity classification (Critical, High, Medium, Low).
   d. Affected asset(s) with asset owner.
   e. Relevant identifiers: CVE, CWE, vendor advisory, CVSS score.
   f. Initial remediation guidance.
   g. Supporting evidence: screenshots, logs, scanner output.
3. Auto-enrichment (where integrations exist):
   a. Fetch CVE details and CVSS score from vulnerability databases.
   b. Map affected asset to asset owner from the CMDB or asset inventory.
   c. Attach known remediation guidance from vendor advisories or knowledge base.
4. Assignment workflow:
   a. Tickets auto-assigned based on asset ownership mapping: code vulnerabilities → development team, infrastructure vulnerabilities → infrastructure/DevOps, cloud misconfigurations → cloud/platform team, network → network team.
   b. If auto-assignment fails (no asset owner mapping), route to the security team for manual triage.
   c. Assignment SLA timer starts when the ticket is created. SLA targets: Critical → 4 hours, High → 24 hours, Medium → 3 business days, Low → 5 business days.
5. Acknowledgment:
   a. Assignee must acknowledge the ticket (not just have it assigned) within the assignment SLA. Acknowledgment means the assignee has reviewed the finding and confirmed they will address it.
   b. If not acknowledged within SLA: (i) escalate to team lead, (ii) if still unacknowledged after an additional SLA period, escalate to Security Officer.
### Alternative Approaches
> **💡 Why you might choose differently:**
> - **Deduplication and correlation before ticket creation:** Instead of creating a ticket for every scanner finding, run findings through a correlation engine that deduplicates (same CVE on 500 hosts = one ticket with a host list, not 500 tickets) and groups related findings. This prevents ticket flooding and ensures remediation efforts are coordinated.
> - **Risk-based ticket creation threshold:** Don't create tickets for Low-severity findings that present negligible risk. Aggregate them into a monthly "Low Severity Findings" report. This prevents the ticketing system from being overwhelmed with low-priority noise.
### Common Pitfalls
> **⚠️ Watch out:**
> - **Tickets created but never assigned.** Auto-created tickets with no owner land in a default queue that nobody monitors. The auto-assignment mapping must be maintained and tested. A ticket older than 7 days in an unassigned state triggers an alert to the Security Officer.
> - **Duplicate tickets for the same finding.** If three scanners detect the same misconfiguration and each creates a ticket, the remediation team wastes time triaging duplicates. Implement deduplication logic based on asset + CVE/finding type. Close duplicates with a reference to the primary ticket.
> - **Ticket template that asks for information nobody has at creation time.** If the template requires fields that the scanner doesn't provide, ticket creation fails or the ticket is created with blanks. Design the template with only the mandatory minimum fields; enrich after creation.

## Procedure 2: SLA Tracking and Escalation
### Standard Approach
1. Configure the ticketing system with SLA definitions:
   a. Assignment SLA: time from ticket creation to assignee acknowledgment (Critical: 4 hours, High: 24 hours, Medium: 3 business days, Low: 5 business days).
   b. Remediation SLA: time from ticket creation to verified closure (Critical: 7 days, High: 30 days, Medium: 90 days, Low: 365 days).
2. SLA monitoring dashboard:
   a. Real-time view accessible to all remediation stakeholders showing: open tickets by severity, SLA status (on-track, approaching breach, breached), aging tickets, and tickets by team.
   b. Color coding: green (on-track), yellow (approaching breach - within 20% of SLA deadline), red (breached).
   c. Dashboard is displayed on a shared monitor in the security operations area and reviewed at weekly team meetings.
3. SLA breach escalation:
   a. **Approaching breach (yellow):** Automated notification to the assignee and their team lead. "Ticket X will breach its SLA in Y days."
   b. **Breached (red, first occurrence):** Escalate to team lead and Security Officer. Assignee must provide a remediation plan with revised target date within 2 business days.
   c. **Breached (red, second escalation - 2x SLA):** Ticket is escalated to the Security Oversight Committee agenda. Executive sponsor is assigned. The finding is treated as a control deficiency and added to the POA&M.
4. SLA metrics reporting:
   a. Weekly: SLA adherence percentage by team (tickets closed within SLA / total tickets closed).
   b. Monthly: SLA trends - is adherence improving or declining? Top breach contributors by team and by finding type.
   c. Quarterly: Comprehensive report to the Security Oversight Committee.
### Alternative Approaches
> **💡 Why you might choose differently:**
> - **SLA based on risk score, not just severity.** A High-severity vulnerability on a publicly accessible critical asset may warrant a shorter SLA than the standard 30 days. Calculate a risk score (severity × asset criticality × exploitability) and map risk score ranges to SLA tiers. This is more nuanced than severity-only SLA assignment.
> - **ServiceNow or Jira Service Management SLA engine:** If using an ITSM platform with native SLA engines, configure SLAs there rather than in external dashboards. The platform tracks SLA timers, sends automated notifications, and provides out-of-the-box SLA reporting.
### Common Pitfalls
> **⚠️ Watch out:**
> - **SLA that pauses when the ticket is "waiting for vendor."** A ticket waiting for a vendor patch for 45 days is still an open risk. The SLA clock should not pause indefinitely. Define a maximum pause duration (e.g., 30 days). If the vendor hasn't released a patch by then, implement compensating controls (mitigation) and revisit the SLA.
> - **SLA adherence that's gamed.** "We met the SLA by closing 50 tickets this week" - but 30 were closed as "won't fix" without proper risk acceptance. SLA metrics must differentiate between: resolved, mitigated, transferred, and accepted. Only resolved and mitigated should count as "met SLA."

## Procedure 3: Verification Testing
### Standard Approach
1. After the remediation team marks a finding as resolved, verification must be performed before the ticket is closed. Verification is NOT performed by the person who implemented the fix - it's an independent check.
2. Verification method by finding type:
   a. **Vulnerability (patch applied):** Re-scan the affected asset with the same scanner that identified the vulnerability. Confirm the vulnerability is no longer reported.
   b. **Misconfiguration:** Re-run the configuration assessment (CSPM scan, compliance check) against the asset. Confirm the misconfiguration is resolved.
   c. **Code fix:** Re-run the SAST/DAST scan. For Critical findings, perform a targeted manual code review or penetration test of the specific finding.
   d. **Process/control gap:** Review updated documentation, interview the control owner, collect evidence that the new process is being followed.
   e. **Mitigation (compensating control):** Test the compensating control: attempt the exploit path and confirm the control blocks it. For a WAF rule, send a test payload and verify it's blocked.
3. Verification documentation:
   a. Record the verification method, date, result, and verifier identity.
   b. Attach verification evidence: scan report showing the vulnerability is gone, configuration screenshot showing the corrected setting, test log showing the blocked exploit attempt.
   c. If verification fails: re-open the finding, return to the remediation team with specific feedback on what was not resolved.
4. Closure:
   a. After successful verification, close the ticket. The status transitions to "Verified Closed."
   b. The closure record includes: resolution applied, verification method, verification date, and verifier.
   c. Closed tickets are retained for the audit evidence retention period.
### Alternative Approaches
> **💡 Why you might choose differently:**
> - **Automated verification (closed-loop remediation):** For findings that can be verified through automated scanning, configure the scanner to automatically re-test and close the ticket when the finding is no longer detected. Human review is reserved for Critical findings and findings where automated verification is not reliable.
> - **Sampling-based verification for bulk findings:** When a patch fixes the same vulnerability on 500 servers, verify a representative sample (e.g., 10% with a minimum of 10 servers, including a mix of OS versions and roles). If the sample passes, assume the fix is effective across the population. Document the sampling methodology.
### Common Pitfalls
> **⚠️ Watch out:**
> - **Verification that checks the wrong thing.** "I verified the patch is installed" → the vulnerability is still present because the service wasn't restarted after the patch. Verification must confirm the vulnerability is gone, not just that the fix was applied.
> - **Self-verification.** The person who applied the patch also verifies it. This creates a conflict of interest and often results in superficial verification. Independent verification (by a different person or automated system) is required.
> - **Verification that takes so long the SLA is breached while waiting.** The verification step should be prompt - within 2 business days for Critical/High, 5 business days for Medium/Low. If the security team is a bottleneck, train remediation teams on self-service verification for common finding types (e.g., "re-scan and attach the report").

## Procedure 4: Exception Management and Reporting
### Standard Approach
1. Exception request process:
   a. When a finding cannot be remediated within SLA, the remediation owner submits an exception request BEFORE the SLA breaches.
   b. The request includes: finding identifier, reason remediation cannot meet SLA, compensating controls in place, proposed revised target date, and business justification.
   c. Approval thresholds: Low/Medium → Security team, High → Security Officer, Critical → Executive leadership.
   d. Approved exceptions are logged in the exception register with the revised target date.
   e. The original SLA timer is paused; a new timer based on the revised target date begins.
2. Exception tracking:
   a. Maintain an exception register: all active exceptions with finding detail, justification, compensating controls, approval authority, revised target date, and review date.
   b. Exceptions are reviewed at each Security Oversight Committee meeting. Any exception approaching its revised target date without resolution is escalated.
   c. Exceptions cannot be renewed indefinitely. After two renewals (third exception period), the finding must be escalated to executive leadership for a strategic decision: allocate resources to fix it, accept the risk permanently, or redesign the affected system.
3. Risk acceptance (alternative to exception):
   a. If the finding will never be remediated (remediation cost exceeds risk, no practical fix exists), follow the formal risk acceptance process per the Risk Assessment Policy.
   b. Accepted risks are tracked in the risk register, not the exception register.
   c. Accepted risks are reviewed quarterly by the Security Oversight Committee.
4. Reporting:
   a. Weekly operational report: open findings, SLA status, new exceptions, approaching deadlines.
   b. Monthly management report: SLA adherence, exception inventory, aging findings, team performance.
   c. Quarterly executive report: trends, systemic issues (recurring finding types), risk acceptance inventory, exception aging.
   d. Critical findings are reported immediately upon discovery - not waiting for the next reporting cycle.
### Alternative Approaches
> **💡 Why you might choose differently:**
> - **Integrated GRC platform for exception workflow:** If using a GRC platform, manage exceptions within the platform rather than a separate register. The platform links exceptions to the original finding, tracks approvals, and includes exceptions in automated reporting.
> - **Exception pre-approval for known scenarios:** Pre-approve exception criteria for common scenarios: "vendor patch ETA exceeds SLA by up to 30 days with compensating WAF rule" is pre-approved. This eliminates approval delays for predictable exception types.
### Common Pitfalls
> **⚠️ Watch out:**
> - **Exception that becomes permanent without review.** An exception granted in Q1 with a revised target of Q2, then extended to Q3, then Q4... and then it becomes de facto accepted without formal risk acceptance. Every exception must have a hard limit on renewals (maximum 2) before escalation to formal risk acceptance.
> - **Reporting that buries bad news.** "90% SLA adherence" sounds good, but if the 10% breaches are all Critical findings on production systems, the aggregate number is misleading. Reports must break down SLA adherence by severity. Critical SLA breaches get a dedicated section.
> - **Exception register that nobody reviews between committee meetings.** The quarterly committee review is not enough. The Security Officer reviews the exception register monthly and escalates any exception that has deteriorated (compensating control failed, revised date approaching without progress) immediately.
