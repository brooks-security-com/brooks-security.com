When people talk about "the West," they usually mean a single, ancient civilization with a clean bloodline running from Athens to the present, one coherent inheritance that belongs to us and marks us off from everyone else. I want to push back on that. The overlap between Western countries is real, and I am not going to pretend otherwise. Legal systems descended from Roman law, a Christian inheritance, liberal-democratic institutions, market economies, a shared canon of art and philosophy: all of that is genuinely there. But the overlap is younger, more arbitrary at its edges, and far less uniquely its own than the word implies (Appiah, 2016). Here is why.

## 1. The Boundary Tracks Power, Not Culture

If shared culture were really the test, the map would follow the culture. It doesn't. Greece is the West's favorite ancestor, yet it spent centuries inside the Ottoman world. Turkey checks many of the same boxes and almost never gets counted. Poland, Hungary, and the Baltics were "Eastern Europe" all through the Cold War and became "the West" the moment they joined NATO and the EU, with no change to their actual heritage. Latin America inherited Roman law, Catholic Christianity, European languages, and Enlightenment constitutions, which is most of the checklist, and still gets left off. Meanwhile Japan and South Korea share the West's economic and political institutions more closely than several countries that are waved straight through. When a category sorts places by their alliances and wealth instead of the culture it claims to be about, that is a political line with a cultural justification bolted on after the fact (Appiah, 2016).

## 2. The Dense Overlap Is Recent

The sameness that feels obvious and lived-in is mostly modern. Shared markets, a common political vocabulary, the same films and universities and academic debates, the dominance of English, the reflex of treating these nations as one bloc: nearly all of it is post-1945. It was built by concrete institutions, NATO, the Bretton Woods system, and the European project, plus the gravitational pull of American power. Two hundred years ago a Sicilian farmer, a Scottish weaver, and a Bavarian priest did not experience themselves as one civilization, and in the ways that actually structured their lives, they weren't. The thick "Western" sameness people point to is the product of recent entanglement, not an ancient shared soul. Reading it back into antiquity is where history quietly turns into flattery (Appiah, 2016). Even the academic version of the idea is a recent build: the unified "Western Civilization" survey course was first assembled at Columbia University in 1919, designed to help students think through the problems of the postwar present rather than to trace an ancient bloodline (Columbia College, n.d.; "Core Curriculum," n.d.).

## 3. The Deep Past Doesn't Cooperate Either

Even the old inheritance is full of holes the myth likes to skip. Knowledge of Greek largely collapsed in Western Europe after Rome fell, and Latin Christendom held only a thin slice of the Greek tradition for centuries. Byzantium, the Greek-speaking Christian survival of the Roman Empire that lasted until 1453, was arguably the more direct heir of both Rome and Greek learning, and the standard story drops it without much comment. A great deal of the Greek philosophy and science that Europe later claimed was preserved and advanced in the Arabic-speaking world, translated wholesale under the Abbasid caliphs in Baghdad between the eighth and tenth centuries, developed by thinkers like Ibn Sina and Ibn Rushd, and fed back into Latin Europe only in the twelfth century through Toledo and Sicily (Gutas, 1998; Saliba, 2007). The words "algebra" and "algorithm" are fossils of that debt. The inheritance was a story of ruptures and borrowing, not an unbroken line.

## 4. The Shared Traits Aren't Uniquely Western

Grant the strongest version of the argument: Western countries really do share rule of law, scientific inquiry, individual rights, and representative government. The problem is that the word "the West" implies these are ours and nobody else's, and that just isn't true. Rule of law, sophisticated legal codes, market economies, scientific reasoning, and rich philosophical traditions show up across human societies, and several of the achievements claimed as Western were inherited from or built alongside non-European cultures. The features are real. The fence that says "these belong to us and separate us from everyone else" is the invented part. A trait that humanity keeps reinventing in many places cannot define one special inheritance.

## 5. Watch Who Needs the Monolith

It is worth asking who the unified version actually serves, because someone always finds it handy. Conservative politicians across what they like to call "Western societies" reach for it constantly. Trump's 2017 Warsaw speech asked whether "the West has the will to survive" and called on the audience to "preserve our civilization in the face of those who would subvert and destroy it" (Trump, 2017). Viktor Orban warns that "the West will fall" and casts Hungary as the last line of defense for a Christian Europe under siege (Al Jazeera, 2019). In this rhetoric the civilization is always singular, always inherited rather than built, and always under threat from outsiders. Every complication in this piece conveniently disappears, because a heritage that turns out to be tangled and shared is a lot harder to wave as a flag.

The clearest example today is Ben Shapiro and The Daily Wire, who have built a sizable business on defending the monolith. In his bestseller The Right Side of History, Shapiro argues that the West stands on two foundations, "Jerusalem and Athens," meaning Judeo-Christian faith and Greek reason, and that these merged into a single inheritance that runs unbroken to the present and is now in decline because we have abandoned it (Shapiro, 2019). This is precisely the bloodline I have been taking apart, presented as established fact. It holds together only by omitting the inconvenient parts: Aristotle returned to Europe through Arabic translation, the unified "Western" package was assembled in the twentieth century, and its boundary has always tracked power rather than culture. The framing also travels. In 2025, Shapiro addressed CPAC Hungary and praised Viktor Orban's government as a defender of Western civilization (Shapiro, 2025), which is a strong signal that the category was never really about Athens at all. At that point it is not a civilization being defended. It is a brand being sold.

## 6. The Civilizing Mission Never Retired

It is also worth being clear about what this kind of talk has historically licensed, because the lineage matters. The impulse is older than the concept itself. Long before anyone packaged "Western civilization" as a single inheritance, European empires justified conquest through the civilizing mission, the claim that Christian, civilized nations had a duty to impose their norms on peoples they dismissed as primitive ("Civilizing mission," n.d.). When the unified idea of the West was finally assembled in the twentieth century, it did not create that impulse. It inherited it and gave it a cleaner name. And the stated goals of progress and uplift consistently coexisted with slavery, plunder, and massacre, which is a reliable sign that the civilization talk was serving another purpose.

That purpose did not disappear when the formal empires ended. Modern colonialism is mostly economic: resource extraction, debt and structural adjustment, interventions launched in the name of "freedom" and "civilization," and the persistent claim that the rest of the world owes its progress to Western guidance. The language that once ranked people in order to rule them now ranks them in order to manage them. So when a politician or a pundit insists that Western civilization is a single precious thing under threat, that is the tradition they are standing in, whether they realize it or not.

## In Summary

Pull on these threads and "the West" does not vanish. What dissolves is the strong version, the distinct and ancient civilization with a shared essence running unbroken from the Acropolis to today. What is left is more accurate and a lot more honest: a cluster of countries with overlapping institutions and culture, an overlap that is real but recent, drawn along lines that follow power as much as heritage, and built largely out of borrowed and widely shared materials. That version explains the same facts without the mythology, and it has the added benefit of being true. You don't take "the West" apart by denying the overlap. You take it apart by looking at the overlap closely and noticing it is younger, more arbitrary, and less its own than the grand word wants you to believe.
